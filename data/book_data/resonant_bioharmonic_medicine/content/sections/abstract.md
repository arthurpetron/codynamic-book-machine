This paper introduces a novel, mechanistically grounded framework for using acoustic and vibrotactile stimuli as direct interventions in human physiology: **Resonant Bioharmonic Medicine (RBM)**. Drawing from recent advances in biophysics, neuroscience, and cellular biomechanics, I argue that sound can be used not merely for emotional modulation, but as a *biological compiler* — a tool to entrain, amplify, or re-harmonize specific metabolic and neural processes at their natural frequencies.

The foundation of RBM is a layered model of the human body as a system of coupled oscillators, from intracellular organelles to neural field dynamics. I provide a structured overview of published studies linking specific frequency bands (e.g. 40 Hz, 0.8 Hz, 100 Hz) to measurable physiological outcomes, including reduced tau accumulation, improved HRV, enhanced sleep-phase memory consolidation, and cytoskeletal modulation.

Building on this evidence, I propose the design of localized “frequency medicine” devices that apply phase-locked, symbolic, or biologically matched vibratory stimuli directly to the body. I outline multiple low-risk, high-impact prototypes that can be constructed immediately using off-the-shelf components, including vagal nerve acoustic entrainment, sleep-aligned audio fields, and mitochondrially-resonant stimulation pads.

This paper concludes by framing RBM as a bridge between bioelectromagnetic physiology, symbolic cognition, and intentional systems design — with potential implications for neurodegenerative disease, trauma recovery, and perceptual engineering.
